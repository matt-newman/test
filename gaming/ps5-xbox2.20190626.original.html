GENERATION GAP

PS5 and Xbox 2 just ‘more of the same’ says top Japanese games boss

  • Alex Sassoon Coby, Digital Gaming Editor
  • 19 Jun 2019, 11:54
  • Updated: 19 Jun 2019, 15:26

THE leap to the next generation of consoles won't contain anything that is "disruptive or super innovative" according to a top Japanese video game boss.

Atsushi Inaba is currently a senior producer at Platinum Games, a studio that is basically a supergroup of acclaimed Japanese developers who formed their own studio after successes with hits such as Resident Evil, Devil May Cry, Ace Attorney and more.

Talking to Video Games Chronicle at E3 last week, Inaba was pretty dismissive of what Xbox Project Scarlett and the next PlayStation would bring to the table for consumers.

He said it was "hard to get excited" about what the new consoles had to offer, especially given they both seem to be using the same chips.

AMD says the version of the Navi chips used in the PS5 will have Sony's "special sauce" while we know the Xbox 2 will have hardware-accelerated ray-tracing - but at their heart they are fundamentally the same thing, and are based off technologies that are already available off the shelf.

Describing the next generation, he told VGC that: “It’s OK. And by that I mean, I’m sure that things will move faster, graphics will be better and maybe it will be easier with less wait times… that’s good for the consumer.

“But it’s more of the same, quite frankly, compared to previous generations. It’s nothing that’s disruptive or super innovative, if you ask me.”

PS5 footage shows games loading in under a second thanks to new tech

Consoles previously used technology that just wasn't found in PCs, but developer's problems making games for the PS3 and Microsoft's approach to try and bring things closer to PC with the Xbox killed this off.

What he says is backed up by what both Sony and Microsoft have said in the run-up to the new consoles launch too.

Sony's bosses have been clear when talking to investors for several years that they want the transition to the PS5 to be as smooth as possible, and recently said they expect the PS4 to drive profits for several years to come.

While "most" of Sony's studios were already working on PS5 games early this year, there are discussions ongoing about getting these games out on PS4 too.

Halo Infinite is launching alongside Xbox Project Scarlett next year, but will be playable on Xbox One consoles too.

Talking to Eurogamer, Xbox Game Studios boss Matt Booty says Microsoft wants "to make sure people who own an Xbox One get a game which runs well up and down the Xbox family. But it plays best on Scarlett."

Xbox 2 news and rumours – what we know so far

Here's what we know about the next-gen Xbox...

  • In June 2019, Microsoft revealed that its all-new console would be coming out in 2020
  • It doesn't have an official name yet - for now, Microsoft are still using the development code-name Project Scarlett
  • As with the next PlayStation, it's going to use super speedy 'solid state' storage to drastically reduce or even totally eliminate loading times
  • This means your games should start up almost instantly, and there won't be lengthy pauses between scenes, or when moving from one part of a game's world to another
  • It's going to be able to play older games too, with Microsoft making big promises about backwards compatibility
  • "Thousands of games across four console generations will look and play best on Project Scarlett," they say, referring to the original Xbox, the Xbox 360, the Xbox One and the new console
  • The third area where they matched Sony is the chips at the console's heart - the brains of Project Scarlett is the same AMD Navi technology at the heart of Sony's next console
  • It's not quite identical to the PlayStation chips, as both are custom-made for the companies in question
  • AMD promised Sony's "special sauce" was built into their chips, while Microsoft describe the core of their new box is a "custom-designed AMD processor"
  • Xbox boss Phil Spencer also revealed that processor would have hardware dedicated to ray-tracing
  • Ray-tracing is a new technology just making it into the most expensive gaming PCs that makes lighting and sound incredibly realistic by calculating the exact path of each ray of light or sound wave through transparent objects and off reflective surfaces
  • This gives you much more realistic lighting and reflections as well as much more natural looking materials and objects
  • Sony has confirmed the next PlayStation will have something similar, but hasn't revealed if the console will have hardware specifically dedicated to it
  • Finally, Microsoft also revealed that Halo: Infinite, which is first revealed at the same briefing last year, is going to launch with the new Xbox
  • The latest chapter in the story of Master Chief will release alongside Project Scarlett devices in time for "Holidays 2020", meaning around mid-November
Control E3 trailer shows what PS5 and Xbox 2 graphics will look like

Some in the industry are suggesting that launch of the new consoles could end up being similar to the launch of the Xbox One X and PS4 Pro, rather than full-on next-generation launches.

This could be good news for gamers, as it means you'll be able to carry on playing the newest games on your PS4 or Xbox One for some time.

It would also be good for the bottom line of companies across the games industry.

Games released at the very start of a new generation often don't use the new hardware to its full potential, are more expensive to make as developers aren't used to the new hardware, and often don't sell as well initially as games released later because not as many gamers own the hardware needed to play it on.
